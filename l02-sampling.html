Sampling & Quantisation

How the analogue world becomes digital — the science of sampling rates, the Nyquist theorem, quantisation noise, and the ADC/DAC pipeline that powers all modern digital systems.

📐 Nyquist Theorem
🎛️ ADC/DAC

1. Sampling — The First Step of Digitisation

Core Concept

What Is Sampling?

Sampling is the process of converting a continuous-time (analogue) signal into a discrete-time sequence by recording the signal's amplitude at regular time intervals. The time between samples is the sampling period Ts, and the number of samples per second is the sampling frequency fs.

Ts = 1 / fs Where: Ts = Sampling period (seconds) fs = Sampling frequency (Hz = samples/second)
💡

Analogy: Imagine a film camera. It doesn't capture smooth, continuous motion — it captures individual frames (samples) at a fixed rate (fps = samples/second). If you shoot at 24fps and the action is slow, it looks fine. But shoot a fast-spinning fan at 24fps and you might see it spinning backwards — that's aliasing.

2. Aliasing — The Sampling Hazard

Critical Concept

⚠️ What Is Aliasing?

Aliasing occurs when a signal is sampled at a rate that is too low (undersampling). The sampled data becomes ambiguous — it could represent multiple different continuous-time signals. The lower-frequency signal that "impersonates" the true signal is called the alias.

Once aliasing occurs, the original signal cannot be perfectly recovered — the information is permanently lost. This is why choosing the correct sampling rate is critical.

🎬

Real-world example: A car wheel spinning at 600 RPM filmed at 24fps may appear to spin slowly backwards. The camera is "under-sampling" the rotation, so the wheel's true frequency aliases to a lower apparent frequency.

3. Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem

⭐ Most Important

⭐ The Theorem

A continuous-time analogue signal with maximum frequency component fmax can be perfectly reconstructed from its samples if and only if the sampling frequency satisfies:

fs ≥ 2 · fmax The minimum sampling rate (2·fmax) is called the NYQUIST RATE. The maximum allowable signal frequency (fs/2) is the NYQUIST FREQUENCY.

If fs < 2·fmax, aliasing occurs and the original signal cannot be recovered.

📘 Worked Example

Problem: Music contains frequency components up to 20 kHz. What is the minimum sampling frequency to reconstruct the audible content?

Given: fmax = 20 kHz Apply: fs ≥ 2 · fmax fs ≥ 2 × 20,000 Hz fs ≥ 40,000 Hz = 40 kHz Answer: Minimum sampling rate = 40 kHz Note: CD audio uses 44.1 kHz — slightly above minimum for practical filter implementation headroom.

4. Quantisation

ADC Step 2

What Is Quantisation?

After sampling (which handles the time axis), quantisation handles the amplitude axis. A digital system can only store discrete amplitude levels — quantisation maps the continuous amplitude to the nearest available digital level.

With a W-bit ADC, there are 2W quantisation levels available.

Key Quantisation Formulas

L = 2^W Where W = ADC word length (bits) L = number of quantisation levels
Δ = (V_max - V_min) / (L - 1) = Range / (2^W - 1)

📘 Worked Example

Problem: A 3-bit ADC covers a range of 0 to 2V. Find the step size.

W = 3 bits L = 2³ = 8 levels Range = 2 - 0 = 2 V Intervals = L - 1 = 7 Δ = 2 / 7 ≈ 0.286 V

Quantisation Noise (Error)

Since continuous amplitudes must be rounded to the nearest level, the difference between the true value and the quantised value is quantisation noise (also called quantisation error). The maximum error is ±Δ/2.

|e_q| ≤ Δ/2 To reduce quantisation noise → increase W (more bits) Every additional bit halves the step size and adds ~6 dB of dynamic range.

5. Signal Reconstruction (DAC)

Digital → Analogue

Reconstruction via Interpolation

Converting a digital signal back to analogue requires reconstructing a continuous waveform from the discrete samples. This is done via interpolation. The simplest method in a DAC is the Zero-Order Hold (ZOH).

Zero-Order Hold (ZOH)

The DAC holds each sample value constant until the next sample arrives. The result is a "staircase" approximation of the original signal.

x_ZOH(t) = x[n] for nTs ≤ t < (n+1)Ts

A low-pass filter is applied after the ZOH to smooth out the staircasing effect and recover the original signal.

6. Resolution and Dynamic Range

ADC Performance

Resolution

The smallest change in analogue voltage that the ADC can detect — equal to the step size Δ.

Resolution = Δ = V_range / (2^W - 1)

Higher W → smaller Δ → better resolution → less quantisation noise.

Dynamic Range (DR)

The ratio of the largest to the smallest representable signal level, expressed in dB.

DR ≈ 6.02 × W dB (Approximately 6 dB per bit) Examples: 8-bit ADC → ~48 dB DR 16-bit ADC → ~96 dB DR 24-bit ADC → ~144 dB DR

Question 03 / 10
Aliasing is a phenomenon that occurs when:
A is correct: Under-sampling causes high-frequency components to "fold" into the lower frequency range, making them indistinguishable from genuine low-frequency content. This frequency overlap is aliasing. Once it happens, the original signal is irrecoverable.
• B is clipping/saturation, not aliasing.
• C is a quantisation issue, separate from sampling.
• D (oversampling) wastes resources but does NOT cause aliasing.
Question 04 / 10
How many quantisation levels does a 4-bit ADC have?
D is correct: Levels = 2^W = 2^4 = 16. Each bit doubles the number of levels: 1-bit = 2 levels, 2-bit = 4, 3-bit = 8, 4-bit = 16.
• A (4) confuses bits with levels — W=4 is the number of bits.
• B (8) is for a 3-bit ADC.
• C (12) has no power-of-2 basis for 4 bits.
Question 05 / 10
A 3-bit ADC covers a voltage range of 0 to 7V. What is the step size Δ?
B is correct: W=3 → L=2³=8 levels → 7 intervals. Δ = Range/(L−1) = 7/(8−1) = 7/7 = 1.0 V.
• A (0.875) = 7/8 — incorrectly divides by L rather than (L−1).
• C (1.4) = 7/5 — incorrect divisor.
• D (7) is the full range, not the step.
Question 06 / 10
The Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) in a DAC produces which characteristic output waveform?
C is correct: ZOH holds each sample's amplitude constant until the next sample arrives, producing a step-like staircase output. A reconstruction filter (LPF) is then applied to smooth it.
• A is the desired final output after filtering, not the raw ZOH output.
• B describes first-order hold (FOH), which linearly interpolates between samples.
• D is the sampled signal before reconstruction, not ZOH output.
Question 07 / 10
Approximately how many dB of dynamic range does each bit of ADC resolution add?
A is correct: DR ≈ 6.02 × W dB. Each additional bit doubles the number of levels (×2 in amplitude = ×4 in power), and 10·log₁₀(4) ≈ 6.02 dB. A 16-bit ADC has DR ≈ 96 dB; adding one bit gives ≈102 dB.
• B (3 dB) corresponds to a factor of 2 in power — that's one more level in voltage (×√2), not one bit.
• C and D do not match the standard formula.
Question 08 / 10
What is the consequence of oversampling (sampling well above the Nyquist rate)?
D is correct: Oversampling is safe — it doesn't cause aliasing or information loss. The trade-off is more samples per second → more data to store and process. However, it spreads quantisation noise across a wider band (noise shaping benefit) and allows the use of simpler, cheaper anti-aliasing low-pass filters.
• A is wrong — aliasing only happens from under-sampling.
• B is wrong — oversampling preserves all information.
• C is wrong — more bits reduce quantisation noise, not more samples.
Question 09 / 10
Quantisation noise refers to:
B is correct: Quantisation noise (error) = actual_value − quantised_value. Its maximum magnitude is ±Δ/2. It is an inevitable consequence of rounding amplitudes to discrete levels.
• A is thermal (Johnson) noise — a physical hardware noise.
• C confuses sampling noise with quantisation noise; sampling itself (in the ideal case) introduces no noise.
• D describes aliasing, which is a time-domain sampling issue.
Question 10 / 10
The Nyquist frequency for a system with a sampling rate of 48 kHz is:
C is correct: Nyquist frequency = fs/2 = 48,000/2 = 24,000 Hz = 24 kHz. This is the maximum signal frequency the system can represent without aliasing. Any signal component above 24 kHz would need anti-aliasing filtering before sampling.
• A (96 kHz = 2×fs) — that's twice the sampling rate, which has no standard name here.
• B confuses Nyquist frequency with sampling frequency.
• D (12 kHz = fs/4) has no standard significance.
